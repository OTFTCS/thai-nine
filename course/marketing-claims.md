# Marketing Claims — Research-Backed Differentiators

Every claim below is grounded in published research. Use for landing pages, social media, course descriptions, and promotional materials.

## How to use this document

- **Lead with the experience**, not the jargon. Say what the learner feels, not what theory it implements.
- **Cite research subtly**: "based on research from [institution]" not "implements Swain's Output Hypothesis."
- **Never use**: SLA, CLT, i+1, EPI, or other academic acronyms in learner-facing copy.

---

## Core Claims

### 1. Pronunciation coaching in every lesson

**Claim:** "Every lesson includes dedicated pronunciation coaching — we teach your mouth, not just your memory."

**What we do:** Every lesson from L002 onwards includes a pronunciation beat with minimal pairs, tone drills, and physical mouth descriptions. Thai tones are trained through contrast and repetition, not just labeled.

**Research:** Thai tones are the #1 challenge for Western learners (NIU Thai Language Learning Framework). Tone errors correlate with script competence (ERIC EJ1280917). Minimal pair training is the gold standard for phonemic awareness.

---

### 2. You speak in every lesson

**Claim:** "You don't just listen — you speak. 40%+ of every lesson is YOUR voice."

**What we do:** At least 40% of drill time requires learner production (substitution, response-building, pause-and-produce). Every lesson includes points where learners say the line before the answer appears.

**Research:** Swain's Output Hypothesis (1995, 2005) — producing language forces deeper cognitive processing than input alone. Izumi (2002) confirmed output + enhanced input > input alone. Conti (2025) ranks Pushed Output Tasks #3 of top 10 techniques.

---

### 3. Built-in spaced repetition

**Claim:** "We bring back what you learned at scientifically-proven intervals — so it sticks."

**What we do:** Every lesson automatically includes vocabulary from 1, 3, 6, and 8 lessons back, woven naturally into drills and roleplay (not forced flashbacks).

**Research:** Roediger & Karpicke (2006) — retrieval practice significantly improves long-term retention vs re-reading. Pavlik & Anderson (2005) — spaced retrieval strengthens memory consolidation. Conti (2025) ranks Spaced Retrieval Practice #1 of top 10 techniques.

---

### 4. Every word in 4+ contexts

**Claim:** "Every new word appears in 4+ different contexts so it sticks — not just once on a flashcard."

**What we do:** Each new vocabulary item appears across explanation, drills, roleplay, and recap. Input flood ensures the target form is encountered repeatedly in varied meaningful contexts.

**Research:** Ellis (2002) — frequency effects play a vital role in language acquisition. Schmidt (2001) — learners need conscious attention to linguistic features for intake. Conti (2025) ranks Input Flood + Focus on Form #2 of top 10 techniques.

---

### 5. 7 proven teaching techniques per lesson

**Claim:** "Every lesson uses 7 proven teaching techniques — not just listen-and-repeat."

**What we do:** Each lesson includes: listen-first modeling, echo/call-and-response, discrimination/choice tasks, substitution drills, pause-and-produce moments, micro-roleplay, and retrieval recap. These are distributed across the lesson, not dumped in one section.

**Research:** Based on Conti's (2025) top 10 research-backed instructional techniques; Multimedia Learning Principles (Mayer); Communicative Language Teaching (CLT).

---

### 6. Real conversations from lesson 1

**Claim:** "Real conversations from lesson 1 — not textbook dialogues nobody actually says."

**What we do:** Every lesson includes a 6+ line micro-roleplay solving one real communicative problem. Lines follow naturally, questions get answers, and the scene has a plausible real-world situation.

**Research:** Communicative Language Teaching — tasks should solve genuine communicative problems. Long's (1996) Interaction Hypothesis — meaningful communication facilitates acquisition.

---

### 7. Designed for 95% understanding

**Claim:** "Lessons are designed so you understand 95%+ of what you hear — the sweet spot for learning."

**What we do:** Max 5-7 new items per lesson, 2-3 per section. 95% comprehensibility rule ensures the learner is never overwhelmed.

**Research:** Krashen's Comprehensible Input hypothesis. Conti (2025) — input must be 95-98% comprehensible for acquisition. Cognitive Load Theory (Sweller) — pre-recorded lessons without live feedback are especially vulnerable to overload.

---

### 8. Structured like a private tutor

**Claim:** "Structured like a private tutor session, not a YouTube explainer."

**What we do:** Pre-recorded interactive format with deliberate response windows, teaching cues ("your turn", "say it before I show you"), and scaffolded progression from notice to produce.

**Research:** Multimedia learning principles (Mayer) — signalling, active viewing, redundancy avoidance. Pre-recorded lesson design research (Foley et al.).

---

### 9. Complete curriculum, zero to confident

**Claim:** "180 lessons from absolute zero to confident conversation — the complete path."

**What we do:** 18 modules, CEFR A0 to B2, covering greetings through professional communication. No gaps in the progression.

**Research:** CEFR (Common European Framework of Reference) and ACTFL proficiency alignment.

---

### 10. Thai the way it actually works

**Claim:** "We explain Thai the way it actually works — not through English grammar rules."

**What we do:** Translation-first for concrete content (direct gloss + usage line). Conceptual anchors only for high-risk items where direct translation would teach the wrong instinct. Forms are taught with their social job, not just their dictionary meaning.

**Research:** Usage-based linguistics. Functional/pragmatic language teaching.
