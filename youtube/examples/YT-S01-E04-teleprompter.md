Chatuchak Weekend Market in Bangkok is the world's largest weekend market. 35 acres, over 15,000 stalls, and more than 200,000 visitors every single weekend. And here's the thing — 70% of those visitors are Thai locals. This is a real market, not a tourist trap. And at a real market, haggling is completely normal. Two phrases you need to know:
แพงไปค่ะ
ลดได้ไหมคะ
Here's the thing — foreigners are always surprised that haggling is completely normal in Thailand. It's not rude. At a market like Chatuchak, the vendor expects it. If you don't haggle, you're probably paying more than you need to.
Today I'm going to teach you 8 essential phrases for shopping at a Thai market. We'll go through each one, I'll explain what it means and how to use it, then you'll hear them all together in a real haggling conversation, and finally we'll practise together.

## VOCAB CARD

เท่าไหร่

---

## VOCAB EXPLAIN

Our first word is เท่าไหร่ — thâo-rài. This means 'how much'. It's the most important question you'll ask at any market. The key thing to know is that in Thai, the question word goes at the end, not the beginning. So instead of 'How much is this?', you say 'This one — how much?' — อันนี้เท่าไหร่. You'll use this one all day long.
กระเป๋าใบนี้เท่าไหร่คะ
That means 'How much is this bag?' See how เท่าไหร่ is right at the end? That's the pattern — item first, then how much.

---

## VOCAB CARD

OK, next word.
แพง

---

## VOCAB EXPLAIN

แพง — phaeng. This means 'expensive'. Short, one syllable, easy to remember. But the really useful version is แพงไป — 'too expensive'. ไป here doesn't mean 'go', it means 'too' or 'excessively'. So แพงไป is your signal to the vendor that you want to negotiate. It's polite, it's normal, and every Thai person will understand exactly what you mean.
โรงแรมนี้แพงมากเลย
That means 'This hotel is really expensive.' แพง after the thing you're describing, then มาก to intensify it. But at the market, you'll mostly just say แพงไปค่ะ — short and to the point.

---

## VOCAB CARD

This next one is your haggling phrase.
ลดได้ไหม

---

## VOCAB EXPLAIN

ลดได้ไหม — lót dâai mǎi. This is 'can you reduce the price?' Let me break it apart: ลด means 'reduce' or 'lower', ได้ means 'can' or 'able to', and ไหม is the yes/no question particle — it turns any statement into a question. So you're literally asking 'lower, can?' It sounds direct but it's perfectly polite. At a market, this is the phrase that starts the negotiation.
ถ้าซื้อสองอัน ลดได้ไหมคะ
That means 'If I buy two, can you reduce the price?' You can level up your haggling by adding a condition before ลดได้ไหม — it gives the vendor a reason to say yes.

---

## VOCAB CARD

OK, next — the opposite of แพง.
ถูก

---

## VOCAB EXPLAIN

ถูก — thùuk. This means 'cheap'. But here's something I always warn my students about: ถูก has two meanings. At the market, it means cheap. But in other contexts, ถูก means 'correct' or 'right'. So if someone says ถูกแล้ว — that means 'that's right', not 'that's cheap'. Context saves you — if you're talking about prices, it means cheap. If you're answering a question, it means correct.
ที่นี่ถูกกว่าห้างมาก
That means 'Here is much cheaper than the mall.' ถูกกว่า means 'cheaper than' — กว่า is how you make comparisons in Thai. You'll hear vendors say things like this to persuade you.

---

## VOCAB CARD

Next — to buy.
ซื้อ

---

## VOCAB EXPLAIN

ซื้อ — súue. This means 'to buy'. It's a rising tone — your voice goes up, like you're asking a question even though you're not. Put it before what you're buying: ซื้อผลไม้ means 'buy fruit'. You'll also hear it in vendor pitches — they'll say something like ซื้อสามใบ ถูกกว่า, meaning 'buy three, it's cheaper'. That's actually one of our dialogue lines today.
อยากซื้อมะม่วงสักอัน
That means 'I'd like to buy a mango.' อยาก means 'want to', so อยากซื้อ is 'want to buy'. สักอัน softens it — a little like saying 'just one'.

---

## VOCAB CARD

This next one is the pair of ซื้อ.
ขาย

---

## VOCAB EXPLAIN

ขาย — khǎai. This means 'to sell'. You'll see it on signs everywhere — ขาย on its own means 'for sale'. ซื้อ and ขาย are a natural pair: buy and sell. At markets you might see signs like ขายถูก — 'selling cheap' — or ขายส่ง — 'wholesale'. Knowing both sides of the transaction makes you sound much more fluent.
ร้านนี้ขายผ้าไหม
That means 'This shop sells silk.' ขาย goes right before what's being sold, same structure as ซื้อ. Notice ผ้าไหม — phâa mǎi — means 'silk'. ผ้า is fabric, ไหม is silk.

---

## VOCAB CARD

OK, this next one is really important for markets — it's a classifier.
ใบ

---

## VOCAB EXPLAIN

ใบ — bai. This is a classifier. In Thai, when you count things, you can't just say a number — you need a special counting word that matches the type of object. ใบ is used for bags, leaves, tickets, and flat or hollow items. So if you want two bags, you say สองใบ, not just สอง. This is one of the biggest grammar differences from English, but once you get the pattern it becomes second nature.
ขอถุงพลาสติกสองใบค่ะ
That means 'Can I have two plastic bags, please?' ถุง is bag, สองใบ is two (of bag-type things). The classifier ใบ always comes after the number.

---

## VOCAB CARD

And our last word — really handy when you're choosing between colours.
สี

---

## VOCAB EXPLAIN

สี — sǐi. This means 'colour'. At a market, you'll use it when you want a specific colour of something. ขอสีแดง means 'I'd like the red one' — ขอ is 'I'd like', สี is 'colour', and แดง is 'red'. Some useful colours: สีแดง is red, สีน้ำเงิน is blue, สีเขียว is green, สีเหลือง is yellow. When there are multiple colours on a stall, just point and say the colour after สี.
มีสีอื่นไหมคะ
That means 'Do you have other colours?' มี is 'have', สีอื่น is 'other colour', ไหม is the question particle. A great phrase when you want to see more options before you decide.

---

## EXPLAIN

อันนี้เท่าไหร่คะ. อัน is a general classifier for small items — อันนี้ means 'this one'. เท่าไหร่ is 'how much'. คะ is the polite particle for women. Notice the question word is at the end — 'this one how much?' In English we'd say 'How much is this?' but in Thai, the question word always comes last. That's a pattern you'll see over and over again.

## BREAKDOWN

สามร้อยบาทค่ะ

---

## EXPLAIN

สามร้อยบาทค่ะ. สาม is three, ร้อย is hundred, บาท is baht — the Thai currency. Numbers in Thai stack very logically: three-hundred-baht, in that order, just like English. ค่ะ is the polite ending for women — the vendor is being polite here. You'll get used to hearing prices this way very quickly.

---

## BREAKDOWN

> *Back to the customer voice — a little playful.*

แพงไปค่ะ ลดได้ไหมคะ

---

## EXPLAIN

แพงไปค่ะ ลดได้ไหมคะ. Two phrases in one sentence — you've got 'too expensive' followed directly by 'can you reduce it?' That's exactly how Thai people would say it: state the problem, then make the ask. Notice ไป after แพง means 'too' here, not 'go'. And ไหม at the end of ลดได้ไหม is what makes it a question. Without ไหม, it's just a statement.

---

## BREAKDOWN

> *Vendor voice — counter-offer.*

สองร้อยห้าสิบค่ะ

---

## EXPLAIN

สองร้อยห้าสิบค่ะ. Two-hundred-fifty — สอง is two, ร้อย is hundred, ห้า is five, สิบ is ten. So ห้าสิบ literally means 'five-ten' which is fifty. Thai numbers build like this: you multiply rather than say 'fifty' as one word. It sounds complicated but once you've counted in Thai a few times, it clicks fast.

---

## BREAKDOWN

> *Customer choosing — decisive.*

ขอสีแดงสองใบค่ะ

---

## EXPLAIN

ขอสีแดงสองใบค่ะ. ขอ is 'I'd like' or 'may I have' — a polite way to request something. สีแดง is 'red colour' — สี plus แดง. Then สองใบ is 'two' with the classifier ใบ for bag-type items. Notice the order: I'd like — colour-red — two-items. In English we'd say 'two red ones' but in Thai the colour comes before the quantity.

---

## BREAKDOWN

> *Vendor pitching — friendly upsell.*

ซื้อสามใบ ถูกกว่าค่ะ

---

## EXPLAIN

ซื้อสามใบ ถูกกว่าค่ะ. ซื้อ is 'buy', สามใบ is 'three items' — three with the classifier ใบ. Then ถูกกว่า — cheap-more, meaning 'cheaper'. กว่า is how you make comparisons in Thai: ถูกกว่า is cheaper, แพงกว่า is more expensive. The vendor is using your own vocabulary against you here — and honestly, it works.
