Thailand has over 70 million PromptPay accounts — in a country of about 70 million people. PromptPay is the QR-code payment system, and it's everywhere. Even a market lady selling 20-baht mangoes will have a QR code taped to a piece of cardboard. But here's the thing — for foreigners visiting Thailand, you mostly won't be using PromptPay. You'll be using cash, sometimes card, and you'll need to know how to ask for the bill, split it, and tip the right way. Two phrases to start:
เก็บเงินด้วยค่ะ
หารกันนะ
One thing that surprises a lot of my students: Thais don't really tip in restaurants the way Americans do. Most sit-down places add a 10% service charge to the bill, so it's already built in. The real tipping move in Thailand is rounding up — taxi drivers, motorbike riders, hairdressers — you let them keep the small change.
Today I'm going to teach you 8 phrases for paying — at a restaurant, splitting the bill with a friend, choosing between card and cash, and tipping a taxi driver. We'll go through each one, I'll explain how and when to use it, then you'll hear them all together in a real conversation, and finally we'll practise.

## VOCAB CARD

เก็บเงิน

---

## VOCAB EXPLAIN

Our first word is เก็บเงิน — gèp ngern. Literally it means 'collect the money'. It's the most natural way to ask for the bill at a Thai restaurant. You'll add ด้วย and a polite particle on the end: เก็บเงินด้วยค่ะ. The server understands instantly. This is what locals say at noodle shops, food courts, even fancier restaurants. It's the everyday version.
ขอเก็บเงินที่โต๊ะนี้ด้วยค่ะ
That means 'Could we have the bill at this table, please.' ที่โต๊ะนี้ is 'at this table' — useful when the server is across the room and you want to flag exactly where to come.

---

## VOCAB CARD

OK, next one — same idea, different vibe.
เช็คบิล

---

## VOCAB EXPLAIN

เช็คบิล — chék-bin. You can hear the English in there, right? Check… bill. It's a loanword. Thais use it interchangeably with เก็บเงิน, but it tends to feel slightly more upmarket. You'll hear it in cafes, brunch places, modern restaurants — more than at a street stall. Same pattern: add ด้วย and a polite particle.
เช็คบิลด้วยครับ ผมรีบ
That means 'Check, please. I'm in a hurry.' ผม is the male word for 'I'. รีบ means 'to hurry'. Useful add-on if you actually need to leave quickly.

---

## VOCAB CARD

Next — the splitting word.
หาร

---

## VOCAB EXPLAIN

หาร — hǎan. This is literally the Thai word for 'divide' — it comes from maths class. But Thais use it for splitting the bill too. The most common form is หารกัน — divide together — let's split it. You can also say หารครึ่ง to split in half. Younger Thais split bills more and more, but heads up: if there's a senior person at the table, they might insist on paying for everyone. And the polite move is to let them.
หารกันสามคนนะ
That means 'Let's split it three ways.' สามคน is 'three people'. The นะ at the end softens it into a friendly suggestion — not a command.

---

## VOCAB CARD

And paired with หาร — the per-person word.
คนละ

---

## VOCAB EXPLAIN

คนละ — khon lá. This means 'per person' or 'each'. คน is 'person', ละ here means 'each' or 'per'. So คนละสองร้อย is 'two hundred each'. Put it together with หาร and you have the full splitting move: หารกันนะ คนละสองร้อย — 'let's split it, two hundred each'. You'll use this constantly when calculating your share.
คนละห้าสิบบาทพอ
That means 'Fifty baht each is enough.' พอ at the end means 'enough'. A useful sign-off when you've worked out a fair per-person amount and want to confirm it.

---

## VOCAB CARD

OK, payment methods. First one — card.
บัตร

---

## VOCAB EXPLAIN

บัตร — bàt. A card. Could be a credit card, debit card, ID card, even a gift card. At a checkout, the key question is รับบัตรไหม — 'do you accept card?' Most mid-range and upmarket restaurants in Thailand take cards. But small noodle stalls and street food usually don't. My advice: always have some cash in your wallet, even in Bangkok.
ที่นี่จ่ายบัตรได้ไหมคะ
That means 'Can I pay by card here?' จ่าย is 'to pay'. ได้ไหม turns it into 'can I…?' Polite, clear, the kind of thing you'd say walking up to the counter.

---

## VOCAB CARD

And the other half of that pair — cash.
เงินสด

---

## VOCAB EXPLAIN

เงินสด — ngern sòt. Cash. เงิน is 'money', สด means 'fresh'. Put them together: cash. At street markets, food carts, and small shops, cash is still king. Quick tip from someone who's spent a lifetime in Thailand: carry small bills. If your meal costs 60 baht and you hand over a 1000-baht note, the vendor will look at you funny — and probably walk to a neighbouring stall to break it.
ขอจ่ายเงินสดได้ไหมคะ
That means 'Can I pay in cash?' Sometimes useful at modern restaurants where the default assumption is card or QR — this signals you're going old-school.

---

## VOCAB CARD

Now we move to the tipping side. This first word is the change you get back.
ทอน

---

## VOCAB EXPLAIN

ทอน — thaawn. The change. The money you get back when you've paid more than the price. But the really useful version is ไม่ต้องทอน — 'no need for change' — which is how Thais tip a taxi driver, a motorbike rider, or a small vendor. You're saying 'keep the difference'. This is THE standard Thai tipping move. Thais don't tip in restaurants the way Americans do, but they round up like this all the time.
ทอนเหลือยี่สิบบาทค่ะ
That means 'Twenty baht in change for you.' เหลือ means 'remaining'. A vendor might say this to you when handing back your change — listen for the number that comes after.

---

## VOCAB CARD

And our last word — the warmer way to tip.
เก็บไว้เลย

---

## VOCAB EXPLAIN

เก็บไว้เลย — gèp wái loei. Literally 'keep it'. It's the warmer, friendlier version of ไม่ต้องทอน. If your taxi was 95 baht and you hand over 100, you can say เก็บไว้เลย — keep it. The เลย at the end gives it a casual, friendly feel — like you're brushing it off, not making a big deal. Use it with taxi drivers, motorbike riders, hairdressers, food delivery riders — anyone you want to tip a small amount.
ไม่เป็นไรค่ะ เก็บไว้เลย
That means 'It's fine, keep it.' ไม่เป็นไร is 'it's fine' or 'no worries' — and it pairs perfectly with เก็บไว้เลย. Together they brush off the change without sounding showy.

## BREAKDOWN

> *Customer voice — polite, asking the server.*

ขอเก็บเงินด้วยค่ะ

---

## EXPLAIN

ขอเก็บเงินด้วยค่ะ. ขอ is 'I'd like' or 'may I have' — it makes any request polite. เก็บเงิน is the bill — literally 'collect money'. ด้วย is 'with' or 'as well' — it softens the request, like adding 'please'. ค่ะ is the polite particle for women. So the full structure is request-marker + the thing + softener + politeness. That ขอ … ด้วยค่ะ pattern works for almost any polite request in Thailand.

---

## BREAKDOWN

> *Switch to server voice — slightly more formal.*

ทั้งหมดสามร้อยหกสิบบาทค่ะ

---

## EXPLAIN

ทั้งหมดสามร้อยหกสิบบาทค่ะ. ทั้งหมด means 'in total' — useful word, you'll hear it at every bill, every checkout. Then the number: สามร้อย is three hundred, หกสิบ is sixty. Thai numbers stack just like English does — three-hundred-sixty. บาท is baht. ค่ะ — polite particle. Notice the structure: total-amount-currency-politeness. That's how a Thai server delivers a bill almost every time.

---

## BREAKDOWN

> *Customer turning to friend — relaxed, friend-to-friend register, no polite particle.*

หารกันนะ คนละร้อยแปดสิบ

---

## EXPLAIN

หารกันนะ คนละร้อยแปดสิบ. Two phrases in one breath, very Thai. หารกัน is 'divide together' — let's split. นะ softens it into a suggestion, not a command. Then คนละ — per person — followed by the number ร้อยแปดสิบ, one-hundred-eighty. Notice there's no ค่ะ here — that's because Nine is talking to her friend, not the server. With friends, you drop the polite particle. Using it would actually feel a bit cold.

---

## BREAKDOWN

> *Customer back to server — polite question.*

รับบัตรไหมคะ

---

## EXPLAIN

รับบัตรไหมคะ. รับ means 'to accept' or 'to receive'. บัตร is 'card'. ไหม is the yes/no question particle — it turns any statement into a question. So literally 'accept card — yes/no?' And คะ at the end is the polite particle for women asking a question. Notice it's คะ with high tone here, not ค่ะ with falling tone — that switch from ค่ะ to คะ when you're asking a question is one of the trickiest details for English speakers.

---

## BREAKDOWN

> *Server voice — apologetic but matter-of-fact.*

รับเงินสดอย่างเดียวค่ะ

---

## EXPLAIN

รับเงินสดอย่างเดียวค่ะ. รับ — accept. เงินสด — cash. อย่างเดียว — 'one type only', meaning 'only'. So 'accept cash only'. อย่างเดียว is a really useful phrase outside of paying too — 'one thing only'. You'll hear it in markets, restaurants, anywhere there's a limit. And notice the server stays polite with ค่ะ even when delivering not-great news.

---

## BREAKDOWN

> *Customer to taxi driver now — friendly, casual.*

ร้อยนึง ไม่ต้องทอนค่ะ

---

## EXPLAIN

ร้อยนึง ไม่ต้องทอนค่ะ. ร้อยนึง is the casual way to say one hundred — full form is หนึ่งร้อย, but in everyday speech it gets shortened to ร้อยนึง. Then ไม่ต้องทอน — 'no need to give change'. ไม่ต้อง is 'don't have to' or 'no need'. ทอน is the change. Together: keep it. This is exactly the rounding-up tip move I mentioned at the start. The taxi was 95 baht, you give 100, they keep 5. Tiny tip, totally normal.
